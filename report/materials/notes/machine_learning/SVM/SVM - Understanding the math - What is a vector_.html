What is a vector?

If we define a point A(3,4)A(3,4) in R2R2 we can plot it like this.

Figure 1: a point

Definition: Any point x=(x1,x2),x≠0x=(x1,x2),x≠0, in R2R2 specifies a vector in the plane, namely the vector starting at the origin and ending at x.

This definition means that there exists a vector between the origin and A.

Figure 2 - a vector

If we say that the point at the origin is the point O(0,0)O(0,0) then the vector above is the vector OA→OA→. We could also give it an arbitrary name such as  uu.

Note: You can notice that we write vector either with an arrow on top of them, or in bold, in the rest of this text I will use the arrow when there is two letters like OA→OA→ and the bold notation otherwise.

Ok so now we know that there is a vector, but we still don't know what IS a vector.

Definition: A vector is an object that has both a magnitude and a direction.

We will now look at these two concepts.

1) The magnitude

The magnitude or length of a vector xx is written ∥x∥‖x‖  and is called its norm.
For our vector OA→OA→,   ∥OA∥‖OA‖ is the length of the segment OAOA

Figure 3

From Figure 3 we can easily calculate the distance OA using Pythagoras' theorem:

OA2=OB2+AB2OA2=OB2+AB2

OA2=32+42OA2=32+42

OA2=25OA2=25

OA=25−−√OA=25

∥OA∥=OA=5‖OA‖=OA=5

2) The direction

The direction is the second component of a vector.

Definition : The direction of a vector u(u1,u2)u(u1,u2) is the vector  w(u1∥u∥,u2∥u∥)w(u1‖u‖,u2‖u‖)

Where does the coordinates of  ww  come from ?

Understanding the definition

To find the direction of a vector, we need to use its angles.

Figure 4 - direction of a vector

Figure 4 displays the vector u(u1,u2)u(u1,u2) with u1=3u1=3 and u2=4u2=4

We could say that :

Naive definition 1: The direction of the vector uu is defined by the angle θθ with respect to the horizontal axis, and with the angle αα with respect to the vertical axis.

This is tedious. Instead of that we will use the cosine of the angles.

In a right triangle, the cosine of an angle ββ is defined by :

cos(β)=adjacenthypotenusecos(β)=adjacenthypotenuse

In Figure 4 we can see that we can form two right triangles, and in both case the adjacent side will be on one of the axis. Which means that the definition of the cosine implicitly contains the axis related to an angle. We can rephrase our naïve definition to :

Naive definition 2: The direction of the vector uu is defined by the cosine of the angle θθ and the cosine of the angle αα.

Now if we look at their values :

cos(θ)=u1∥u∥cos(θ)=u1‖u‖

cos(α)=u2∥u∥cos(α)=u2‖u‖

Hence the original definition of the vector ww . That's why its coordinates are also called direction cosine.

Computing the direction vector

We will now compute the direction of the vector uu  from Figure 4.:

cos(θ)=u1∥u∥=35=0.6cos(θ)=u1‖u‖=35=0.6

and

cos(α)=u2∥u∥=45=0.8cos(α)=u2‖u‖=45=0.8

The direction of u(3,4)u(3,4) is the vector w(0.6,0.8)w(0.6,0.8)

If we draw this vector we get Figure 5:

Figure 5: the direction of u

We can see that ww as indeed the same look as uu except it is smaller. Something interesting about direction vectors like ww is that their norm is equal to 1. That's why we often call them unit vectors.

The sum of two vectors

Figure 6: two vectors u and v

Given two vectors u(u1,u2)u(u1,u2) and v(v1,v2)v(v1,v2) then :

u+v=(u1+v1,u2+v2)u+v=(u1+v1,u2+v2)

Which means that adding two vectors gives us a third vector whose coordinate are the sum of the coordinates of the original vectors.

You can convince yourself with the example below:

Figure 7: the sum of two vectors

The difference between two vectors

The difference works the same way :

u−v=(u1−v1,u2−v2)u−v=(u1−v1,u2−v2)

Figure 8: the difference of two vectors

Since the subtraction is not commutative, we can also consider the other case:

v−u=(v1−u1,v2−u2)v−u=(v1−u1,v2−u2)

Figure 9: the difference v-u

The last two pictures describe the "true" vectors generated by the difference of uu and vv.

The dot product

One very important notion to understand SVM is the dot product.

Definition: Geometrically, it is the product of the Euclidian magnitudes of the two vectors and the cosine of the angle between them

Which means if we have two vectors xx and yy and there is an angle θθ  (theta) between them, their dot product is :

x⋅y=∥x∥∥y∥cos(θ)x⋅y=‖x‖‖y‖cos(θ)

Why ?

To understand let's look at the problem geometrically.

Figure 12

In the definition, they talk about cos(θ)cos(θ), let's see what it is.

By definition we know that in a right-angled triangle:

cos(θ)=adjacenthypotenusecos(θ)=adjacenthypotenuse

In our example, we don't have a right-angled triangle.

However if we take a different look Figure 12 we can find two right-angled triangles formed by each vector with the horizontal axis.

Figure 13

and

Figure 14

So now we can view our original schema like this:

Figure 15

We can see that

θ=β−αθ=β−α

So computing cos(θ)cos(θ) is like computing cos(β−α)cos(β−α)

There is a special formula called the difference identity for cosine which says that:

cos(β−α)=cos(β)cos(α)+sin(β)sin(α)cos(β−α)=cos(β)cos(α)+sin(β)sin(α)

(if you want you can read  the demonstration here)

Let's use this formula!

cos(β)=adjacenthypotenuse=x1∥x∥cos(β)=adjacenthypotenuse=x1‖x‖

sin(β)=oppositehypotenuse=x2∥x∥sin(β)=oppositehypotenuse=x2‖x‖

cos(α)=adjacenthypotenuse=y1∥y∥cos(α)=adjacenthypotenuse=y1‖y‖

sin(α)=oppositehypotenuse=y2∥y∥sin(α)=oppositehypotenuse=y2‖y‖

So if we replace each term

cos(θ)=cos(β−α)=cos(β)cos(α)+sin(β)sin(α)cos(θ)=cos(β−α)=cos(β)cos(α)+sin(β)sin(α)

cos(θ)=x1∥x∥y1∥y∥+x2∥x∥y2∥y∥cos(θ)=x1‖x‖y1‖y‖+x2‖x‖y2‖y‖

cos(θ)=x1y1+x2y2∥x∥∥y∥ cos(θ)=x1y1+x2y2‖x‖‖y‖ 

If we multiply both sides by ∥x∥∥y∥‖x‖‖y‖ we get:

∥x∥∥y∥cos(θ)=x1y1+x2y2‖x‖‖y‖cos(θ)=x1y1+x2y2

Which is the same as :

∥x∥∥y∥cos(θ)=x⋅y‖x‖‖y‖cos(θ)=x⋅y

We just found the geometric definition of the dot product ! 

Eventually from the two last equations we can see that :

x⋅y=x1y1+x2y2=∑i=12(xiyi)x⋅y=x1y1+x2y2=∑i=12(xiyi)

This is the algebraic definition of the dot product !

 A few words on notation

The dot product is called like that because we write a dot between the two vectors.
Talking about the dot product x⋅yx⋅y is the same as talking about

  • the inner product  ⟨x,y⟩⟨x,y⟩ (in linear algebra)
  • scalar product because we take the product of two vectors and it returns a scalar (a real number)

The orthogonal projection of a vector

Given two vectors xx and yy, we would like to find the orthogonal projection of xx onto yy.

Figure 16

To do this we project the vector xx onto yy

Figure 17

This give us the vector zz

Figure 18 : z is the projection of x onto y

By definition :

cos(θ)=∥z∥∥x∥cos(θ)=‖z‖‖x‖

∥z∥=∥x∥cos(θ)‖z‖=‖x‖cos(θ)

We saw in the section about the dot product that

cos(θ)=x⋅y∥x∥∥y∥cos(θ)=x⋅y‖x‖‖y‖

So we replace cos(θ)cos(θ) in our equation:

∥z∥=∥x∥x⋅y∥x∥∥y∥‖z‖=‖x‖x⋅y‖x‖‖y‖

∥z∥=x⋅y∥y∥‖z‖=x⋅y‖y‖

If we define the vector uu as the direction of yy then

u=y∥y∥u=y‖y‖

and

∥z∥=u⋅x‖z‖=u⋅x

We now have a simple way to compute the norm of the vector zz.
Since this vector is in the same direction as yy it has the direction  uu

u=z∥z∥u=z‖z‖

z=∥z∥uz=‖z‖u

And we can say :

The vector z=(u⋅x)uz=(u⋅x)u is the orthogonal projection of xx onto yy.

Why are we interested by the orthogonal projection ? Well in our example, it allows us to compute the distance between xx and the line which goes through yy.

Figure 19

We see that this distance is ∥x−z∥‖x−z‖

∥x−z∥=(3−4)2+(5−1)2−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−√=17−−√‖x−z‖=(3−4)2+(5−1)2=17

The SVM hyperplane

Understanding the equation of the hyperplane

You probably learnt that an equation of a line is : y=ax+by=ax+b. However when reading about hyperplane, you will often find that the equation of an hyperplane is defined by :

wTx=0wTx=0

How does these two forms relate ?
In the hyperplane equation you can see that the name of the variables are in bold. Which means that they are vectors !  Moreover, wTxwTx is how we compute the inner product of two vectors, and if you recall, the inner product is just another name for the dot product !

Note that

y=ax+by=ax+b

is the same thing as

y−ax−b=0y−ax−b=0

Given two vectors  w⎛⎝⎜−b−a1⎞⎠⎟w(−b−a1) and x⎛⎝⎜1xy⎞⎠⎟x(1xy)

wTx=−b×(1)+(−a)×x+1×ywTx=−b×(1)+(−a)×x+1×y

wTx=y−ax−bwTx=y−ax−b

The two equations are just different ways of expressing the same thing.

It is interesting to note that w0w0 is −b−b, which means that this value determines the intersection of the line with the vertical axis.

Why do we use the hyperplane equation wTxwTx instead of  y=ax+by=ax+b ?

For two reasons:

  • it is easier to work in more than two dimensions with this notation,
  • the vector ww will always be normal to the hyperplane

    (Note: I received a lot of questions about the last remark. ww will always be normal because we use this vector to define the hyperplane, so by definition it will be normal. As you can see this page, when we define a hyperplane, we suppose that we have a vector that is orthogonal to the hyperplane)

And this last property will come in handy to compute the distance from a point to the hyperplane.

Compute the distance from a point to the hyperplane

In Figure 20 we have an hyperplane, which separates two group of data.

Figure 20

To simplify this example, we have set w0=0w0=0.

As you can see on the Figure 20, the equation of the hyperplane is :

x2=−2x1x2=−2x1

which is equivalent to

wTx=0wTx=0

with w(21)w(21)  and x(x1x2)x(x1x2)

Note that the vector ww is shown on the Figure 20. (w is not a data point)

We would like to compute the distance between the point A(3,4)A(3,4) and the hyperplane.

This is the distance between AA and its projection onto the hyperplane

Figure 21

We can view the point AA as a vector from the origin to AA.
If we project it onto the normal vector ww

Figure 22 : projection of a onto w

We get the vector pp

Figure 23: p is the projection of a onto w

Our goal is to find the distance between the point A(3,4)A(3,4) and the hyperplane.
We can see in Figure 23 that this distance is the same thing as ∥p∥‖p‖.
Let's compute this value.

We start with two vectors, w=(2,1)w=(2,1) which is normal to the hyperplane, and a=(3,4)a=(3,4) which is the vector between the origin and AA.

∥w∥=22+12−−−−−−√=5–√‖w‖=22+12=5

Let the vector uu be the direction of ww

u=(25–√,15–√)u=(25,15)

pp is the orthogonal projection of aa onto ww so :

p=(u⋅a)up=(u⋅a)u

p=(3×25–√+4×15–√)up=(3×25+4×15)u

p=(65–√+45–√)up=(65+45)u

p=105–√up=105u

p=(105–√×25–√,105–√×15–√)p=(105×25,105×15)

p=(205,105)p=(205,105)

p=(4,2)p=(4,2)

∥p∥=42+22−−−−−−√=25–√‖p‖=42+22=25

Compute the margin of the hyperplane

Now that we have the distance ∥p∥‖p‖ between AA and the hyperplane, the margin is defined by :

margin=2∥p∥=45–√margin=2‖p‖=45

We did it ! We computed the margin of the hyperplane !

  41. Beungeut Boloho September 17, 2016 at 11:17 am

    Hi, can you explain what is the bias b visually? Is it the distance of vertical axis to the origin or the distance of the hyperplane to the origin?

    Reply ↓
    1. Alexandre KOWALCZYK Post authorSeptember 17, 2016 at 8:10 pm

      Given an hyperplane having the equation wx+b=0 with vectors w(w0,w1) and x(x0,x1). b is the distance between the vertical axis and the origin only when the value w1 of the weight vector is equal -1. Indeed, when we transform this hyperplane equation to a line equation of the form y=ax+c we get a = -w0/w1 and c = -b/w1. Some books represent b as being the distance between the origin and the hyperplane, but I think this is true only under certain conditions, at least that is what I found when trying to verify it by myself using the first formula of this article using formulas from this page.
